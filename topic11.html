Topic 11: Transfer Learning & Pre-trained Model Adaptation

Why build from scratch when you can build on the shoulders of giants?

1. From Building to Reusing

🚲 → 🏍️ Analogy: Learning to Ride

Imagine you spent months learning to ride a bicycle. You mastered balance, steering, braking, and pedaling. Now someone hands you a motorbike. Do you need to relearn everything from scratch? No!

You already know how to balance, steer, and brake. The only new skills are using the throttle and changing gears. The motorbike problem is easier because you transfer knowledge from cycling.

So far in this course, we've built neural networks from random initialization — every single weight started as noise, and gradient descent sculpted them into useful features. This is like teaching a child to read by starting with zero knowledge of letters, words, or grammar.

⚠️ The Reality Check

In real-world systems, two things are almost always scarce:

  • Labelled data: Getting high-quality labels is expensive and time-consuming (medical imaging, autonomous driving, etc.)
  • Compute resources: Training large models from scratch costs millions of dollars and weeks of GPU time

Starting from random initialization is not just inefficient — it's often impractical.

Definition: Transfer Learning

Transfer learning is the technique of reusing knowledge learned from one problem (source task) to help solve a related problem (target task) faster, with less data, or with better performance.

Instead of training every weight from scratch, we take a model that has already learned useful features and adapt it to our specific task.

This is exactly what transfer learning does: it takes a model that already "knows" how to see edges, textures, shapes, and objects, and asks it to apply that knowledge to your specific problem — whether that's identifying dog breeds, detecting tumors, or classifying manufacturing defects.

💡 Key Insight

Transfer learning changes the game from "collect millions of images and train for weeks" to "collect hundreds of images and train for hours." It's the single most important technique for making deep learning practical in resource-constrained settings.

Transfer learning isn't always the answer. If your target task is very different from any available pre-trained model (e.g., a completely novel sensor modality), the transferred features may be useless or harmful. Also, if you have abundant labelled data and compute, training from scratch might give you full control over the architecture and avoid inheriting any biases from the source model.

2. Why Transfer Works — Hierarchy of Features

🎨 Analogy: The Artist's Toolkit

A trained artist learns skills in layers: first how to hold a brush, then basic strokes, then color mixing, then composition, and finally style. When asked to paint a new subject (say, portraits instead of landscapes), they don't relearn how to hold a brush — they reuse low-level skills and only adapt high-level decisions.

When a deep neural network is trained on a large dataset (like ImageNet with millions of images), it doesn't just memorize the training examples. It learns a hierarchy of increasingly complex features:

Feature Hierarchy in Deep Networks

  • Layer 1-3 (Early/Lower layers): Edges, corners, color gradients, textures — extremely generic features useful for almost any visual task
  • Layer 4-8 (Middle layers): Parts of objects — eyes, wheels, leaves, fabric patterns — somewhat specific but still broadly useful
  • Layer 9-12+ (Deep/Upper layers): Whole objects, faces, specific patterns — highly task-specific features

Transferability Gradient

Transferability(T) ∝ 1 / Specificity(Layer)

Where: Lower layers → high transferability (generic features)
Upper layers → low transferability (task-specific features)

The landmark study by Yosinski et al. (2014) at NYU systematically measured this by training a network on ImageNet, then measuring how well different layers transferred to a new task:

Yosinski et al. (2014) — Key Findings

Experiment: Train a 8-layer CNN on ImageNet (1000 classes), then transfer different layers to a new 8-class task.

Results:

  • Transferring layers 1-2: Task accuracy dropped only 2-3% from fully trained — generic features transfer well
  • Transferring layers 1-6: Task accuracy dropped 5-8% — still mostly useful
  • Transferring layers 1-8 (entire network): Accuracy dropped 15-20% — top layers are too specific

Conclusion: Features evolve from general (bottom) to specific (top). Transfer works because the generic features are reusable; the specific features need to be relearned.

💡 The Rule of Thumb

Re-use bottom layers (generic features), replace or fine-tune top layers (task-specific features). This is the foundational principle behind all transfer learning strategies we'll discuss.

This emerges naturally from the structure of data. Edges, textures, and color gradients are statistically necessary to represent any visual input. A network must learn to detect edges to distinguish any object, regardless of whether the task is cats vs. dogs or tumors vs. healthy tissue. Higher layers combine these building blocks into increasingly specific patterns that depend on the particular task.

3. When Transfer Helps and Hurts

The Two Sides of Transfer

Transfer learning is powerful but not magical. Its success depends on the relationship between source and target tasks. Sometimes it helps dramatically; sometimes it can actually hurt performance.

When Transfer Helps

Transfer learning provides the greatest benefit when:

  • Source and target share underlying structure: If both tasks require detecting edges, textures, or similar patterns, the transferred features are directly useful.
  • Target data is limited: When you have few labelled examples, starting from pre-trained weights prevents overfitting.
  • Target data is "close" to source data: Medical imaging (source: general images, target: X-rays) benefits because visual features transfer. Stock prediction (source: NLP, target: images) would not.

⚠️ Negative Transfer

Negative transfer occurs when borrowed features actually mislead the target model, resulting in worse performance than training from scratch.

This happens when:

  • Source and target domains are very different (e.g., transferring from natural images to spectrograms)
  • The source model has learned biases that are counterproductive for the target task
  • The target task requires fundamentally different feature types

Example: A model pre-trained on satellite imagery might learn features about atmospheric conditions and lighting that are irrelevant (or harmful) when transferred to microscopic cell classification.

🚗 Analogy: Driving Skills

Driving a car transfers well to driving a truck (same road rules, similar controls). But driving a car does not transfer well to piloting an airplane — the environment, controls, and physics are fundamentally different. Forcing car-driving habits onto an airplane could be dangerous (negative transfer).

💡 Key Takeaway

Always check domain similarity before choosing transfer learning. If the domains are very different, consider training from scratch or using unsupervised pre-training on your target domain instead.

4. Formal Definitions

Domain (D)

A domain is defined as D = {X, P(X)} where:

  • X: The feature space (e.g., all possible pixel values for images, or all possible word embeddings for text)
  • P(X): The marginal distribution over features (e.g., how often certain colors, textures, or objects appear)

Task (T)

A task is defined as T = {Y, f(·)} where:

  • Y: The label space (e.g., {cat, dog, bird} for classification, or all real numbers for regression)
  • f(·): The predictive function (the true mapping from inputs to labels that we're trying to learn)

Transfer Learning Problem

Given: Source domain Dₛ = {Xₛ, Pₛ(Xₛ)} and source task Tₛ = {Yₛ, fₛ(·)}
Target domain Dₜ = {Xₜ, Pₜ(Xₜ)} and target task Tₜ = {Yₜ, fₜ(·)}

Goal: Learn the target conditional distribution P(Yₜ|Xₜ) using knowledge from (Dₛ, Tₛ)

Where: Dₛ ≠ Dₜ and/or Tₛ ≠ Tₜ

What May Differ Between Source and Target

Types of Differences

Component Can Differ? Example
Feature spaces (X) ✅ Yes Text vs. images vs. audio
Distributions P(X) ✅ Yes Daytime photos vs. nighttime photos
Label spaces (Y) ✅ Yes Binary vs. multi-class classification
Predictive function f(·) ✅ Yes Object detection vs. image classification

💡 Key Insight

Transfer learning is most powerful (and most common) when the feature space X is the same but the distributions P(X) differ (domain adaptation) or the tasks T differ (task adaptation). The more components that differ, the harder transfer becomes.

5. Taxonomy of Transfer Learning

Transfer learning methods can be classified based on how the source and target domains and tasks relate:

Three Main Categories

Type Domains Tasks Target Labels Example
Inductive TL Same or different Different Available ImageNet → Medical imaging classification
Transductive TL Different Same NOT available Adapting sentiment model from English to French (unlabelled French data)
Unsupervised TL Different Different NOT available Using ImageNet features to improve clustering on medical images

📚 Analogy: University Transfer

  • Inductive: A biology major switches to chemistry. Different subject (task), but some knowledge carries over (same university system = same domain). They have textbooks (target labels) to guide them.
  • Transductive: A student moves from a US university to a UK university, same subject (task), but different educational system (domain). They don't have lecture notes yet (no target labels) but can observe how classes work.
  • Unsupervised: A student switches both university and major, with no syllabus to guide them. They must figure out both the new system and new subject simultaneously.

⚠️ Most Common in Practice

The vast majority of practical transfer learning falls into the inductive category: we have a pre-trained model (source), a different task (target), and at least some labelled target data. The techniques in this topic primarily address inductive transfer learning.

6. Two Core Strategies

There are two fundamental approaches to using pre-trained models:

Strategy 1: Feature Extraction

Idea: Use the pre-trained network as a fixed feature extractor. Freeze all backbone layers and only train a new classification head.

How it works:

  1. Take pre-trained model (e.g., ResNet-50 trained on ImageNet)
  2. Remove the final classification layer
  3. Pass your data through the frozen network → get feature vectors
  4. Train a simple linear classifier (or small MLP) on these features

Characteristics:

  • Few trainable parameters → fast training, hard to overfit
  • Best when target data is small (10-1000 examples)
  • Best when target domain is close to source domain
  • Mathematically: ∂L/∂θⱼ = 0 for all frozen layers j ∈ F

Strategy 2: Fine-Tuning

Idea: Unfreeze some or all layers of the pre-trained model and continue training with a small learning rate.

How it works:

  1. Take pre-trained model
  2. Replace final layer with new task head
  3. Unfreeze selected layers
  4. Continue training with small learning rate (10×-100× smaller than initial training)

Characteristics:

  • More parameters move → higher performance ceiling
  • Higher risk of overfitting and catastrophic forgetting
  • Best when target data is plentiful (>1000 examples)
  • Best when target domain differs more from source

⚠️ Why Small Learning Rate?

The pre-trained weights are already near a good minimum. A large learning rate would push them far from this minimum, potentially destroying the learned features. Think of it as "editing a draft" vs. "writing from scratch" — you make careful, small changes, not wholesale rewrites.

💡 Decision Rule

Feature extraction when data is limited and domains are similar. Fine-tuning when data is plentiful or domains differ. When in doubt, start with feature extraction and graduate to fine-tuning if needed.

7. Mathematics of Freezing & Fine-Tuning

Fine-Tuning Objective

θ* = arg min_θ (1/N) Σᵢ L(f_θ(xᵢ), yᵢ)

Where: θ ← θ_source (initialize from pre-trained weights)

f_θ = neural network with parameters θ

L = loss function (e.g., cross-entropy)

N = number of training examples

Freezing Mathematics

When freezing layers, we set gradients to zero for those parameters:

∂L/∂θⱼ = 0 for all j ∈ F (frozen layer indices)

This means frozen parameters never update: θⱼ remains at θⱼ_source

Only parameters NOT in F are updated via gradient descent.

Discriminative Learning Rates

Different layers receive different learning rates:

θₗ ← θₗ - ηₗ · ∂L/∂θₗ

Where: ηₗ = η · ξ^(L-l)

  • η = base learning rate
  • ξ = decay factor (e.g., 0.9)
  • L = total number of layers
  • l = current layer index

Why Discriminative Learning Rates?

Intuition: Lower layers contain generic features that are already good — they need only tiny adjustments. Upper layers contain task-specific features that need more significant adaptation.

Analogy: When renovating a house, you don't tear down the foundation (low layers) — you make minor adjustments. But you might completely redo the kitchen and bathrooms (high layers).

⚠️ The Overshooting Problem

Pre-trained weights sit near a good minimum in the loss landscape. A large learning rate takes a big step that can jump out of this minimum, destroying the learned features. This is why fine-tuning always uses learning rates 10×-100× smaller than training from scratch.

Think of it like tuning a radio that's already close to the right station — you make tiny adjustments, not big swings.

8. Worked Example — Counting Parameters

Let's see exactly how parameter-efficient feature extraction is in practice.

Scenario: Image Classification with Pre-trained ResNet

Setup:

  • Pre-trained backbone (frozen): ResNet-50 with 23,500,000 parameters
  • Backbone output: 2,048-dimensional feature vector
  • Target task: Classify into 5 classes

Step 1: New Classification Head

The new head is a simple linear layer:

Parameters in head = (input_dim × output_dim) + bias

= (2048 × 5) + 5 = 10,240 + 5 = 10,245 parameters

Step 2: Total Parameters

Total = Backbone (frozen) + Head (trainable)

= 23,500,000 + 10,245 = 23,510,245 parameters

Step 3: Trainable Fraction

Trainable fraction = Trainable / Total

= 10,245 / 23,510,245

= 0.000436 = 0.0436%

Step 4: What This Means

You train just 0.04% of the parameters and inherit 23.5 million frozen parameters "for free." The frozen backbone has already learned to extract useful visual features from millions of images. You're just teaching the model what those features mean for your specific 5-class problem.

💡 The Power of Feature Extraction

With only 10,245 trainable parameters, you can achieve performance that would require training 23.5 million parameters from scratch. This is why feature extraction is so powerful for small datasets — you have very few parameters to overfit, but access to extremely rich features.

If you trained the same 5-class problem from scratch with a randomly initialized ResNet-50:

  • Parameters to train: 23,510,245 (all of them)
  • Data needed: Typically 50,000+ images per class for good performance
  • Training time: Hours to days on GPU

With feature extraction:

  • Parameters to train: 10,245
  • Data needed: As few as 50-500 images per class
  • Training time: Minutes on GPU

10. Adaptation Spectrum

📝 Analogy: The Editing Spectrum

Consider writing a book:

  • Training from scratch: Writing every word yourself (blank page)
  • Feature extraction: Using someone's outline and filling in the details
  • Partial fine-tuning: Taking a draft and revising key chapters
  • Full fine-tuning: Taking a draft and revising the entire manuscript
  • PEFT: Making surgical edits to just the most important paragraphs

The Four Strategies (Ordered by Weight Movement)

  1. Feature Extraction: Freeze backbone, train only new head. Minimum weight movement. Cheapest and most robust.
  2. Partial Fine-Tuning: Unfreeze top layers, keep bottom frozen. Moderate weight movement. Good balance of performance and stability.
  3. Full Fine-Tuning: Unfreeze all layers with small learning rate. Maximum weight movement. Highest ceiling but highest risk.
  4. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT): Add small trainable modules (e.g., LoRA adapters) while keeping original weights frozen. Near-full accuracy at ~1% parameter cost.

Spectrum Visualization

💡 The Golden Rule

Start simple, add complexity as needed. Begin with feature extraction. If performance is insufficient, try partial fine-tuning. Only go to full fine-tuning if you have enough data and evidence that the simpler approaches aren't enough.

11. Catastrophic Forgetting

⚠️ The Core Problem

Catastrophic forgetting occurs when a neural network "forgets" previously learned information while learning new information. During fine-tuning with a large learning rate, weights are pushed far from their pre-trained values, scrambling the general features that made the model useful in the first place.

🧠 Analogy: The Multilingual Brain

Imagine a person who speaks English, French, and Spanish fluently. If they immersion themselves in Japanese without maintaining their other languages, they might lose fluency in French and Spanish. This is catastrophic forgetting — learning new information displaces old knowledge.

Why does this happen? When you fine-tune with a large learning rate:

  1. Weights move far from their pre-trained positions
  2. Generic features (edges, textures) encoded in lower layers get overwritten
  3. The model becomes specialized for the target task but loses general capabilities
  4. Performance on the target task may improve, but the model becomes fragile

Prevention Strategies

  • Small learning rate: Use 10×-100× smaller LR than initial training. This keeps weights near their pre-trained values.
  • Learning rate scheduling: Start small and decay further. Warmup then cosine decay works well.
  • Freeze lower layers: Don't touch the generic features in layers 1-6. Only fine-tune task-specific upper layers.
  • EWC (Elastic Weight Consolidation): Add a penalty that prevents important weights from moving too far from their pre-trained values.
  • Gradual unfreezing: Start by fine-tuning only the head, then gradually unfreeze layers from top to bottom.

💡 Key Insight

Catastrophic forgetting is the primary enemy of fine-tuning. Every strategy we'll discuss (discriminative learning rates, warmup, EWC, PEFT) is designed to combat it. The fundamental principle: protect the general, adapt the specific.

12. Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay (LLRD)

LLRD Formula

ηₗ = η_top · δ^(L-l)

Where:

  • ηₗ = learning rate for layer l
  • η_top = learning rate for the top (task-specific) layer
  • δ = decay factor (e.g., 0.9)
  • L = total number of layers
  • l = current layer index (1 = bottom, L = top)

Worked Example: 12-Layer Transformer

Given: η_top = 1×10⁻³, δ = 0.9, L = 12 layers

Layer 12 (Top — most task-specific):

η₁₂ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9^(12-12) = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9⁰ = 1×10⁻³ × 1.000 = 1.000×10⁻³

Layer 11:

η₁₁ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9^(12-11) = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9¹ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.900 = 9.000×10⁻⁴

Layer 6 (Middle):

η₆ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9^(12-6) = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9⁶ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.531 = 5.314×10⁻⁴

Layer 1 (Bottom — most generic):

η₁ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9^(12-1) = 1×10⁻³ × 0.9¹¹ = 1×10⁻³ × 0.314 = 3.138×10⁻⁴

Summary:

Top layer moves at 1.000×10⁻³
Bottom layer moves at 3.138×10⁻⁴
Bottom moves at ~⅓ the rate of the top

💡 Why This Works

Generic features (bottom layers) need only tiny adjustments — they're already good. Task-specific features (top layers) need more significant adaptation. LLRD automatically allocates more "movement budget" to layers that need it most, while protecting generic knowledge.

The decay factor δ controls how much lower layers are protected:

  • δ = 1.0: No decay — all layers get the same LR (standard fine-tuning)
  • δ = 0.9: Moderate decay — bottom gets ~⅓ of top's LR (common choice)
  • δ = 0.8: Aggressive decay — bottom gets ~⅙ of top's LR (more protection)
  • δ = 0.5: Very aggressive — bottom gets ~0.05% of top's LR (almost frozen)

Most papers use δ ∈ [0.85, 0.95]. Start with 0.9 and adjust based on validation performance.

13. Warmup + Cosine Learning Rate Schedule

This two-phase schedule is the most popular LR schedule for fine-tuning:

Warmup Phase (t ≤ T_w)

η(t) = η_max · (t / T_w)

LR increases linearly from 0 to η_max

Cosine Decay Phase (t > T_w)

η(t) = η_max · ½ · [1 + cos(π · (t - T_w) / (T - T_w))]

LR decays from η_max to 0 following a cosine curve

Worked Example

Given: η_max = 2×10⁻⁵, T_w = 100 (warmup steps), T = 1000 (total steps)

Step 1: During Warmup (t = 50)

η(50) = 2×10⁻⁵ × (50/100) = 2×10⁻⁵ × 0.5 = 1.0×10⁻⁵

At step 50, we're halfway through warmup, so LR is half of maximum.

Step 2: End of Warmup (t = 100)

η(100) = 2×10⁻⁵ × (100/100) = 2×10⁻⁵ × 1.0 = 2.0×10⁻⁵

Warmup complete — LR has reached maximum.

Step 3: Mid-Decay (t = 550)

η(550) = 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + cos(π × (550-100)/(1000-100))]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + cos(π × 450/900)]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + cos(π/2)]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + 0]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × 0.5 = 1.0×10⁻⁵

At the midpoint of decay, LR is exactly half of maximum.

Step 4: End of Training (t = 1000)

η(1000) = 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + cos(π × (1000-100)/(1000-100))]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + cos(π)]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × ½ × [1 + (-1)]

= 2×10⁻⁵ × 0 = 0

LR reaches zero at the end of training.

💡 Why Warmup?

At the start of fine-tuning, gradients can be noisy and large (especially if the target task differs from the source). Warmup prevents the model from taking destructive steps early on. Once gradients stabilize, cosine decay provides a smooth, effective learning rate schedule.

14. LoRA — Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning

LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation

LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) is the most popular PEFT method. Instead of fine-tuning all weights, it adds small trainable matrices while keeping the original weights frozen.

LoRA Decomposition

W = W₀ + ΔW = W₀ + BA

Where:

  • W₀ ∈ ℝ^(d×k) = pre-trained weight matrix (FROZEN)
  • B ∈ ℝ^(d×r) = low-rank matrix (TRAINABLE)
  • A ∈ ℝ^(r×k) = low-rank matrix (TRAINABLE)
  • r << min(d,k) = rank (e.g., 4, 8, 16)

Key insight: The weight update ΔW has low "intrinsic rank" — most of the important information can be captured by a small number of dimensions.

Worked Example: BERT Attention Projection

Setup: BERT-base attention projection matrix W ∈ ℝ^(768×768)

Full Fine-Tuning:

Parameters = 768 × 768 = 589,824 parameters

LoRA with Rank r = 8:

B ∈ ℝ^(768×8), A ∈ ℝ^(8×768)

Parameters = (768 × 8) + (8 × 768) = 6,144 + 6,144 = 12,288 parameters

Comparison:

Ratio = 12,288 / 589,824 = 0.0208 = 2.08%

LoRA uses 48× fewer parameters while achieving near-identical performance!

How It Works:

  1. Initialize A with random Gaussian, B with zeros → ΔW = BA = 0 at start
  2. During training, only A and B are updated
  3. At inference, merge: W_new = W₀ + BA (no extra computation!)

🔧 Analogy: Car Tuning

Full fine-tuning is like rebuilding an entire engine. LoRA is like adding a small performance chip — you keep the original engine intact but add a tiny module that tweaks its behavior. The result is almost as good as a full rebuild, but cheaper, faster, and reversible.

💡 Why LoRA Works

The key insight is that weight updates during fine-tuning have low intrinsic rank. Even though W is 768×768, the actual changes needed are much simpler and can be captured by a small 768×8 × 8×768 decomposition. This is why LoRA achieves 98%+ of full fine-tuning performance with only 2% of the parameters.

  • r = 1-4: Minimal parameters, good for similar source/target tasks
  • r = 8-16: Good balance for most tasks (common default: r=8)
  • r = 32-64: More capacity for dissimilar tasks, approaching full fine-tuning cost
  • Rule of thumb: Start with r=8, increase if validation performance plateaus

Memory savings: LoRA typically uses 10-50× less GPU memory than full fine-tuning.

15. EWC — Elastic Weight Consolidation

EWC: Preventing Catastrophic Forgetting

EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) adds a penalty term that prevents important weights from changing too much during fine-tuning. It measures each weight's importance using Fisher Information.

EWC Loss Function

L_total = L_new + (λ/2) · Σᵢ Fᵢ · (θᵢ - θ*ᵢ)²

Where:

  • L_new = standard loss on target task
  • λ = regularization strength (hyperparameter)
  • Fᵢ = Fisher Information for parameter i (importance measure)
  • θᵢ = current value of parameter i
  • θ*ᵢ = pre-trained (source) value of parameter i

Worked Example: Single Parameter

Given: λ = 0.4, Fᵢ = 50, θ* = 1.20 (pre-trained value), θ = 1.50 (current value)

Step 1: Compute the difference

Δθ = θ - θ* = 1.50 - 1.20 = 0.30

Step 2: Square the difference

Δθ² = 0.30² = 0.09

Step 3: Multiply by Fisher Information

Fᵢ × Δθ² = 50 × 0.09 = 4.5

Step 4: Apply λ/2

penalty = (0.4/2) × 4.5 = 0.2 × 4.5 = 0.9

Interpretation:

The penalty term contributes 0.9 to the total loss. This creates a "spring force" pulling θ back toward θ*. The strength depends on both λ (how much we care about forgetting) and Fᵢ (how important this parameter is).

Two Cases: Important vs. Unimportant Weights

Parameter Fᵢ (Importance) Δθ Penalty Effect
θ₁ (edge detector) 500 (very important) 0.30 0.2 × 500 × 0.09 = 9.0 Strong anchor — barely moves
θ₂ (rarely used) 0.1 (unimportant) 0.30 0.2 × 0.1 × 0.09 = 0.0018 Free to adapt — moves easily

⚓ Analogy: Anchor Points

Imagine weights are boats on a lake. EWC places anchors (springs) on important boats. The anchor strength (Fᵢ) determines how tight the spring is. Important boats (edge detectors, common patterns) are tightly anchored and barely move. Unimportant boats (rarely used features) have weak anchors and can drift freely to adapt to the new task.

💡 Key Insight

EWC allows selective adaptation: parameters crucial for the source task stay anchored, while less important parameters are free to adapt. This is the mathematical implementation of "protect the general, adapt the specific."

💡 The Sweet Spot

LoRA (PEFT) offers the best trade-off for most practical applications: near-full fine-tuning accuracy at a fraction of the cost. It's the default choice in 2024+ for adapting large pre-trained models. Use full fine-tuning only when you have abundant data and compute, and feature extraction only when simplicity is paramount.

The Golden Rule

Always benchmark against a from-scratch baseline.

Even though transfer learning usually wins, you should always verify:

  1. Train a small model from scratch on your target data
  2. Compare against the pre-trained model with your chosen adaptation strategy
  3. If the pre-trained model doesn't help, it may indicate domain mismatch or other issues

Practical Checklist

  • ✅ Check if a pre-trained model exists for your modality (vision, text, audio)
  • ✅ Assess domain similarity (how different is your data from training data?)
  • ✅ Count your labelled examples (this determines your strategy)
  • ✅ Start with the simplest approach (feature extraction)
  • ✅ Monitor validation loss for overfitting
  • ✅ Use learning rate warmup + cosine decay
  • ✅ Apply discriminative learning rates or LoRA if fine-tuning
  • ✅ Consider EWC if learning multiple tasks sequentially
  • ✅ Save checkpoints and compare against baselines

💡 Final Wisdom

Transfer learning has democratized deep learning. You no longer need millions of images and weeks of GPU time to build state-of-the-art models. With a pre-trained model and a few hundred labelled examples, you can achieve remarkable performance. The key is understanding when and how to adapt — and that's what this entire topic has been about.

Flashcards

What is Transfer Learning?
Reusing knowledge learned from one problem (source task) to help solve a related problem (target task) faster, with less data, or with better performance.
What is Negative Transfer?
When borrowed features from the source task actually mislead the target model, resulting in worse performance than training from scratch. Occurs when source and target domains are very different.
Why do lower layers transfer better than upper layers?
Lower layers learn generic features (edges, textures) useful for any task. Upper layers learn task-specific features (faces, objects) that may not apply to a new task. Transferability decreases from generic to specific layers.
What is the key difference between Feature Extraction and Fine-Tuning?
Feature extraction freezes all pre-trained layers and only trains a new head. Fine-tuning unfreezes some/all layers and continues training with a small learning rate. Feature extraction is cheaper but has lower ceiling; fine-tuning is more expensive but more flexible.
What is LoRA and why is it parameter-efficient?
LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) decomposes weight updates as BA where B∈R^(d×r) and A∈R^(r×k) with r<<min(d,k). Only A and B are trained while original weights W₀ are frozen. This captures low-rank updates with ~2% of parameters.
What is Catastrophic Forgetting?
When a neural network forgets previously learned information while learning new information. During fine-tuning with large LR, weights move far from pre-trained values, scrambling general features. Prevented by small LR, freezing lower layers, or EWC.
What does EWC stand for and what does it do?
Elastic Weight Consolidation. It adds a penalty term Ltotal = Lnew + (λ/2)·ΣFᵢ·(θᵢ - θ*ᵢ)² that prevents important weights (measured by Fisher Information Fᵢ) from moving far from their pre-trained values, preventing catastrophic forgetting.
What is a Domain in transfer learning?
A domain D = {X, P(X)} consisting of a feature space X and a marginal distribution P(X) over those features. Domains differ when feature spaces or distributions differ.
Why use discriminative learning rates in fine-tuning?
Lower layers have generic features needing small adjustments (small LR). Upper layers have task-specific features needing larger adaptation (larger LR). ηₛ = η·ξ^(L-l) automatically allocates more movement to upper layers.
What percentage of parameters are typically trainable in feature extraction?
Usually 0.04-5% of total parameters. For example, with a 23.5M parameter backbone and 10K parameter head, only 0.04% are trainable. You inherit millions of frozen parameters for free.

Scenario

Scenario: Medical Image Classification

You're a machine learning engineer at a hospital. Your task is to build a system that classifies chest X-rays into 3 categories: Normal, Pneumonia, and COVID-19.

Constraints:

  • You have only 500 labelled X-rays (150 Normal, 200 Pneumonia, 150 COVID-19)
  • You have access to a pre-trained ResNet-50 (trained on ImageNet — natural images)
  • You have 1 GPU with limited memory
  • You need the model deployed within 2 weeks

Decision Process:

  1. Data size check: 500 examples → very small dataset → feature extraction or LoRA
  2. Domain similarity: ImageNet (natural images) → X-rays (medical images) → somewhat different but visual features (edges, textures) transfer well
  3. Compute constraint: Limited GPU → prefer parameter-efficient methods
  4. Time constraint: 2 weeks → need fast iteration

Recommended Strategy:

Primary: LoRA (PEFT)

  • Use pre-trained ResNet-50 backbone
  • Add LoRA adapters (rank r=8) to the last 2-3 residual blocks
  • Train only LoRA parameters (~2% of total)
  • Use warmup + cosine LR schedule
  • Expected training time: 1-2 hours on single GPU

Backup: Feature Extraction

  • If LoRA overfits (check validation loss), fall back to feature extraction
  • Freeze entire backbone, train only classification head
  • Even fewer parameters, even less overfitting risk

Why NOT Full Fine-Tuning?

  • 500 examples is too few to fine-tune 25M parameters
  • High risk of overfitting and catastrophic forgetting
  • Would require extensive regularization and careful LR scheduling

Expected Outcome:

With LoRA on 500 examples, you can likely achieve 85-92% accuracy — remarkable for such a small dataset. Without transfer learning, a model trained from scratch on 500 examples would likely achieve only 50-65% accuracy (barely above random for 3 classes).

Scenario Key Takeaways

  • Small dataset (< 1000 examples) → feature extraction or LoRA
  • Limited compute → parameter-efficient methods
  • Always have a backup strategy (feature extraction is the safety net)
  • Transfer learning's value is most dramatic with small datasets
  • Monitor validation loss to detect overfitting early
